Detailed Information: Non-Anastomotic Stricture (NAS) Formation

Role of PBG/PVP Damage in NAS:

Damage to PBGs and the PVP is critical in NAS development.[1, 2, 14, 15, 16] NAS are often multiple strictures proximal to the anastomosis, common after IRI, especially in DCD livers.[2, 15, 16, 18, 21, 23]

Mechanisms of Failed Regeneration:

  • Reduced PBG Viability: Livers developing NAS show reduced PBG mass, decreased PBG cell proliferation, and increased apoptosis.[1]
  • Compromised Microenvironment: PVP damage leads to persistent local hypoxia, hampering regeneration.[15, 16]
  • Bile Salt Toxicity & Dysregulated Healing: Delayed repair exposes denuded areas to cytotoxic bile salts, aggravating injury and promoting a fibrotic (rather than regenerative) wound-healing response.[16]
  • Exhaustion/Dysregulation: Initial PBG activation (e.g., ↑VEGF-A [2]) may become overwhelmed by chronic insults (hypoxia, inflammation, bile toxicity), leading to senescence or a shift towards pro-fibrotic signaling.[1, 13, 15, 16]

Predictors and Contributing Factors:

Injury to deep PBGs pre-transplant is a strong NAS predictor.[14] The higher NAS incidence in DCD livers underscores the PBG/PVP complex's vulnerability to severe warm ischemia.[2, 15, 16] Immunological insults (rejection, ABO incompatibility) can also contribute to PBG damage and NAS.[15, 16, 18, 21]
