Detailed Information: CMV Infection

CMV Cholangiopathy:

CMV is the most frequent opportunistic infection post-LT, associated with biliary strictures (anastomotic and NAS).[8, 9, 24, 25, 26] CMV DNA is often more detectable in bile than blood in patients with biliary issues, suggesting localized biliary tract activity.[24]

Mechanisms of Damage:

CMV causes direct viral cytopathic effects (e.g., "owl's eye" inclusions in biliary/PBG epithelium) [9, 10] and indirect damage via immunomodulation, potentially worsening alloimmune responses or creating a pro-inflammatory environment.[27, 28] "Activated" or proliferating PBGs are particularly susceptible targets for CMV replication.[25]

Histopathology & Diagnosis:

Classic CMV infection shows cytomegalic cells with large intranuclear inclusions.[9] PBG glandular epithelium can show these changes.[10] IHC for CMV antigens or ISH/RNAscope for viral nucleic acids confirm infection.[8, 24, 29]

Clinical Impact:

CMV antigenemia is strongly associated with adverse biliary outcomes.[8] Occult CMV in the biliary tract (detectable in bile, not blood) may drive biliary stenosis and graft failure.[24] PBGs could be significant sites of CMV replication and shedding into bile.[11, 24]
