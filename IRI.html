Detailed Information: Ischemia-Reperfusion Injury (IRI)

Pathophysiology of IRI:

IRI results from hypoxia during organ procurement/preservation, followed by reoxygenation that triggers reactive oxygen species (ROS), inflammation, and microcirculatory disturbances.[14, 15, 16] The biliary epithelium is highly vulnerable.[12, 14, 17] DCD livers, experiencing prolonged ischemia, show more PBG damage and are prone to non-anastomotic strictures (NAS).[2, 15, 16, 18]

Histopathological Changes:

IRI causes PBG epithelial disruption, pyknotic nuclei, and distorted morphology.[2] The PVP shows reduced density, endothelial apoptosis, subintimal edema, and arteriolonecrosis.[1, 2, 3]

Molecular Responses & The "Double-Edged Sword":

PBGs upregulate HIF-1α and VEGF-A, attempting repair.[13, 2] However, if the microenvironment is severely compromised (e.g., persistent hypoxia), this proliferative drive might become dysregulated, contributing to fibrosis rather than effective re-epithelialization.[1, 13, 2, 19]

Functional Consequences:

IRI impairs PBG regenerative capacity, leading to reduced PBG mass and increased apoptosis in grafts developing NAS.[1] Pre-transplant injury to deep PBGs (>50% loss) strongly predicts post-transplant NAS.[14]
